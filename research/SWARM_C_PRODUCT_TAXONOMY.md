## Executive Summary

Valentine was told there are 4 products. The backend defines **15 intelligence products** across **4 layers** serving **12 buyer segments**. The real answer: **there are 4 product brands that internally compose 15+ distinct data products**, with the potential to expand to **10+ worker-facing products** as the platform matures.

**The structure:**
- **4 worker-facing brands** (what Valentine knows) — these are the user interfaces
- **15 backend intelligence products** (what the platform produces) — these are the data engines
- **12 buyer segments** (who pays) — these are the revenue channels
- **5 training data products** (what feeds back into the system) — these are the flywheel fuel

The 4 brands are not products — they are **platform surfaces** that compose multiple backend intelligence products into worker-facing experiences. Think of it like Google: one search bar, but hundreds of underlying products (Maps, Shopping, News, Scholar, etc.).

### The Original 4 — Core Platform Products

#### 1.1 Soko Pulse — Market Intelligence
**Tagline:** "Know your market before you walk into it."

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **User** | Traders, mama mbogas, dukawallahs, hawkers, market vendors |

## 4. Training Data Products (What Feeds the Flywheel)

These are data products that improve Msaidizi and the intelligence platform over time. They are not sold — they are the moat.

### 4.1 Voice Training Data
**Source:** Every voice interaction with Msaidizi
**Volume:** 14 dialects, millions of utterances
**What it trains:**
- Dialect-specific speech recognition models
- Swahili NLP models (intent recognition, entity extraction)
- Voice synthesis for reports (natural-sounding Swahili output)
- Code-switching detection (Sheng ↔ Swahili ↔ English ↔ mother tongue)

**Moat value:** First-ever Swahili AI that handles real dialectal variation. No competitor has this data.

### 4.2 Transaction Training Data
**Source:** Every M-Pesa, cash, and mobile money transaction logged
**Volume:** Millions of transactions across thousands of markets
**What it trains:**
- Business pattern recognition (seasonal, weekly, daily cycles)
- Anomaly detection (fraud, price manipulation, supply shocks)
- Demand prediction models
- Cross-selling patterns ("traders who buy X also buy Y")

**Moat value:** The largest dataset of informal economy transactions in Africa. By the time a competitor collects equivalent data, Angavu has years of advantage.

### 4.3 Behavioral Training Data
**Source:** How workers interact with Msaidizi, savings patterns, credit behavior
**Volume:** Behavioral signals from active users
**What it trains:**
- Credit risk models (payment regularity, savings discipline, business consistency)
- Churn prediction (when workers are about to leave)
- Product recommendation models
- Nudge effectiveness (which interventions change behavior)

**Moat value:** Behavioral data for informal workers has never been collected at scale. This powers Alama Score's alternative credit scoring.

### 4.4 Market Training Data
**Source:** Price reports, market observations, supply chain interactions
**Volume:** Daily prices from hundreds of markets
**What it trains:**
- Price forecasting models (ARIMA, LSTM, Prophet)
- Market structure detection (monopolies, cartels, competitive markets)
- Supply chain network models
- Spatial price interpolation (kriging models)

**Moat value:** Real-time informal market price data doesn't exist anywhere else. This is the foundation of Soko Pulse.

### 4.5 Social Training Data
**Source:** Community interactions, group savings (chamas), referral networks
**Volume:** Social graph data from active communities
**What it trains:**
- Social network models for credit scoring
- Community detection algorithms
- Trust propagation models
- Information diffusion models

**Moat value:** Understanding social trust networks in informal economies is unique to Angavu. This powers both Alama Score (social credit features) and Jamii Insights (community intelligence).

## 8. Answer to Valentine's Question

**"Are they really 4 products only?"**

**No.** Here's the truth:

| Layer | Count | What Valentine Sees | What Actually Exists |
|-------|-------|--------------------|--------------------|
| Worker-facing brands | 4 (expandable to 10) | "I have 4 products" | The 4 brands are platform surfaces that compose 15+ backend products |
| Backend intelligence products | 15 | Invisible to workers | These are the data engines — each is independently valuable |
| Derived data products | 13 | Invisible but sold to buyers | These are computed from the 15 and sold as standalone products |
| Buyer segments | 12 | "I sell to a few companies" | Each segment gets a different product bundle |
| Training data products | 5 | Invisible | These are the moat — they make everything better over time |

**The 4 brands are the tip of the iceberg.** Underneath each brand are 3-5 backend intelligence products, each of which can be sold independently to different buyer segments.

**Think of it like this:**
- **Soko Pulse** (worker brand) = Price Indices + Demand Elasticities + Market Structure + Time Series Forecasts + Spatial Analysis
- **Biashara Pulse** (worker brand) = Business Efficiency Scores + Inflation Measures + Demand Elasticities + Causal Impact
- **Alama Score** (worker brand) = Credit Risk Models + Network Analysis + Behavioral Data + Survival Analysis
- **Jamii Insights** (worker brand) = Poverty Measures + Inequality Measures + Network Analysis + Social Data

Each of those backend products is independently sellable to different buyer segments. That's why there are 15 backend products serving 12 buyer segments, not just 4 products.

**The real product count:**
- 10 worker-facing products (4 existing + 6 natural extensions)
- 15 backend intelligence products
- 13 derived data products
- 5 training data products
- **Total: 43 distinct data products**
