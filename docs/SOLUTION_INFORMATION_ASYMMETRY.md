# How Angavu Solves Information Asymmetry

**Problem:** ECO 101 — Workers don't know what they don't know
**Scale:** 600M+ informal workers globally, 18.1M in Kenya
**Academic Root:** Akerlof (1970), "The Market for Lemons"; Stiglitz & Weiss (1981)

---

## THE PROBLEM

Information asymmetry occurs when one party in a transaction has more or better information than the other. In Africa's informal economy, the asymmetry is **total and structural**:

| Information Gap | Who Benefits | Who Loses |
|----------------|-------------|-----------|
| Fair market prices for goods | Middlemen, wholesalers | Mama mboga (vegetable vendor) |
| Competitive interest rates | Digital lenders (15%/month) | Boda boda rider borrowing for fuel |
| Seasonal demand patterns | Established businesses with data | Jua kali artisan (informal metalworker) |
| Policy impact on income | Government, formal sector | 83% of Kenya's workforce |
| True cost of goods sold | Suppliers with market data | Small-scale retailers |
| Business health metrics | No one — the data doesn't exist | Every informal worker |

**Real-world consequence:** Kenya's 2024 Finance Bill protests. People died because they couldn't see what the bill would cost them. The information was available — in policy documents, in economic analyses, in parliamentary records. But not in a language, format, or channel that 18.1M informal workers could access.

## HOW ANGAVU FIXES IT

### Soko Pulse — Real-Time Market Intelligence

**What it does:** Aggregates anonymized transaction data from thousands of Msaidizi users to produce real-time price intelligence for informal markets.

**Before Angavu:**
- Mama mboga buys tomatoes at KSh 80/kg because that's what her supplier charges
- She doesn't know the wholesale price is KSh 50/kg
- She doesn't know tomatoes are KSh 60/kg at the next market
- She doesn't know tomato prices drop 30% every Tuesday when supply peaks

**After Angavu:**
- Msaidizi tells her: "Tomatoes are KSh 55/kg at Gikomba wholesale today"
- "Your supplier is charging 45% above wholesale"
- "Tomato prices typically drop on Tuesdays — consider buying then"
- "The mama mboga 2km away sells tomatoes at KSh 90/kg — your price of KSh 100/kg is 11% above local average"

**The asymmetry collapses.** She now has the same information her supplier has.

**The asymmetry collapses.** She knows her business as well as a formally-educated MBA would.

**The asymmetry collapses.** The worker walks into the lender's office with the same information the lender has.

## THE ECONOMIC MECHANISM

Information asymmetry causes three market failures:

1. **Adverse Selection** (Akerlof, 1970): When buyers can't distinguish quality, they pay average prices. Good sellers exit. Only bad sellers remain. The market degrades.

2. **Moral Hazard** (Stiglitz & Weiss, 1981): When one party can't observe the other's behavior, exploitation becomes rational. Lenders charge maximum rates because they can't assess risk.

3. **Market Failure** (Arrow, 1963): When information is asymmetric, markets fail to allocate resources efficiently. The informal economy operates at massive inefficiency.

**Angavu's intervention:** By providing information to the less-informed party (the worker), Angavu restores market efficiency. Prices converge to fair value. Interest rates converge to risk-adjusted rates. Resources allocate more efficiently.
