## THE PROBLEM

Coordination failure occurs when individuals would benefit from acting collectively but cannot organize to do so. In Africa's informal economy, coordination failure is **pervasive and dangerous**:

| Coordination Failure | Consequence | Example |
|---------------------|-------------|---------|
| Workers can't collectively negotiate prices | Individual exploitation by suppliers | Each mama mboga negotiates alone; suppliers play them against each other |
| Workers can't collectively demand fair lending | Predatory rates persist | Digital lenders charge 15%/month because no individual can push back |
| Workers can't collectively assess policy impact | Protests replace informed civic engagement | 2024 Finance Bill — people died because there was no mechanism to understand the bill collectively |
| Workers can't collectively share market intelligence | Each reinvents the wheel | 100 boda boda riders in the same area all learn the same route economics independently |
| Workers can't collectively lobby for infrastructure | Government ignores informal sector | No data on what 83% of the workforce actually needs |

**The Olson Problem:** Mancur Olson showed that large groups face a free-rider problem — each individual benefits from collective action but has no incentive to contribute. The result: no one organizes, and everyone loses.

**The Ostrom Solution:** Elinor Ostrom showed that coordination is possible when there are clear rules, monitoring mechanisms, and graduated sanctions. But this requires infrastructure — which informal workers don't have.

## THE ECONOMIC MECHANISM

Coordination failure causes:

1. **Collective Action Problem** (Olson, 1965): Large groups can't organize because free-riding is rational. Each individual benefits from collective action but has no incentive to contribute.

2. **Tragedy of the Commons** (Hardin, 1968): Shared resources (market information, bargaining power) are under-provided because no individual has incentive to create them.

3. **Principal-Agent Problem** (Jensen & Meckling, 1976): When workers try to organize, leaders (agents) may not represent workers' (principals') interests.

**Angavu's intervention:** By providing collective intelligence through individual action, Angavu eliminates the coordination cost entirely. There is no group to organize, no leader to corrupt, no free-rider to exploit. Each worker acts individually; the collective benefit emerges automatically.
