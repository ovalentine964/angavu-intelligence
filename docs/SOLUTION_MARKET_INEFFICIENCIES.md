**Problem:** The informal economy operates at massive inefficiency — and workers bear the cost
**Scale:** 83% of Kenya's workforce; 600M+ globally
**Academic Root:** Stigler (1961), "The Economics of Information"; Coase (1937), "The Nature of the Firm"

## THE PROBLEM

Market inefficiency occurs when resources are not allocated to their highest-value use. In Africa's informal economy, inefficiency is **structural and self-reinforcing**:

| Inefficiency | Mechanism | Cost to Workers |
|-------------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Price opacity** | No centralized price discovery for informal goods | Workers pay 20-45% above wholesale |
| **Information costs** | Finding the best price requires time and transport | Workers default to nearest/most familiar supplier |
| **Search frictions** | No marketplace connecting informal buyers and sellers | Each transaction is bilateral with no alternatives |
| **Credit rationing** | Banks can't assess informal workers → exclude them entirely | Workers pay 15%/month to digital lenders vs 1-3% formal rates |
| **Missing markets** | No market for informal worker credit data, insurance, or business analytics | Entire categories of financial services don't exist for 83% of workforce |
| **Transaction costs** | No digital records → every negotiation starts from zero | Repeated information costs for every transaction |
| **Thin markets** | Each worker operates in a market of one | No competition → no price discipline |

**The compound effect:** These inefficiencies don't just cost workers money — they keep the entire informal economy operating below potential. If every mama mboga could access wholesale prices, the aggregate effect on Kenya's GDP would be measurable.

## THE ECONOMIC MECHANISM

Market inefficiency in informal economies stems from:

1. **Search Costs** (Stigler, 1961): Finding the best price requires time, money, and effort. Workers rationally settle for "good enough" rather than optimal.

2. **Transaction Costs** (Coase, 1937): Every market interaction has costs beyond the price of the good — negotiation, verification, enforcement. In informal markets, these costs are high.

3. **Missing Markets** (Arrow, 1963): When information is too costly to gather, entire markets fail to form. Credit for informal workers is the classic example.

4. **Externalities** (Pigou, 1920): Each worker's data has value to other workers. But without a mechanism to share it, the externality is unrealized.

**Angavu's intervention:**
- **Reduces search costs** to near-zero (AI does the searching)
- **Reduces transaction costs** (digital records, pattern recognition)
- **Creates missing markets** (Alama Score creates credit market; Soko Pulse creates price discovery)
- **Internalizes externalities** (federated learning shares data benefits without sharing data)
